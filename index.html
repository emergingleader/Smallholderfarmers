Cross-Country Insights
What the data tells us when we look across Kenya, Rwanda and Malawi together.
💰

Financial behaviour shifts faster than income and it sticks

Across every country and cohort, saving and budgeting behaviour shows the most dramatic and consistent change. Kenya moved from 8% saving always to 88% at endline. Rwanda from 41% to 95%. Malawi started at just 2% and reached 74% at follow-up, holding at 54% at endline. The longitudinal data confirms this is not a post-training spike. Nduti's save always rate was 91% at follow-up and remained at 89% at endline. Momul's budgeting reached 97.7% at follow-up and held at 96% at endline. Rwanda Phase 1 savings grew from £3 at baseline to £12.6 at endline. The programme is rewiring how people relate to money and the evidence holds across time.

📈

Income growth is real and post-peak dips are stabilisation, not failure

The longitudinal data tells an honest story. Rwanda Phase 1 income peaked at £65 at follow-up then settled at £60.5 at endline. Malawi peaked at £84 then came to £76. This is consistent with post-intervention stabilisation, where early behavioural intensity moderates into sustainable routines. The critical signal is that endline figures remain significantly above baseline in every single case across every country.

🌱

Tea dependency is falling everywhere

Nduti leads most aggressively, with tea as primary income dropping from 65% to 35%, meaning for most participants tea is no longer the main earner. Momul dropped from 81% to 53%. Rwanda from 99% to 74%. Malawi spiked to 100% at follow-up before falling to 65% at endline, suggesting participants first deepened tea income before diversifying. The direction is consistent everywhere.

🌊

The ripple effect compounds over time

The ripple effect grows the longer a cohort has been trained. Kenya Phase 1 went ×3 at follow-up to ×5 at endline. Rwanda went ×5 to ×10. Malawi reached ×13 at endline, the highest across all cohorts and countries. Community diffusion is not a one-time training event; it keeps spreading. The older the cohort, the wider the reach.

🎯

Attribution to EL training is near-universal across every context

Across all groups and countries, 90–100% of participants who made changes attributed those changes directly to Emerging Leaders training. Rwanda Phase 1 reached 100%. This consistency across different geographies, cooperative cultures and time points reflects the programme's perceived relevance and practical applicability in genuinely different farming contexts. It also provides strong evidence of additionality, changes that would not have happened without this programme.

🌍

The programme works differently in different contexts - a design strength

Gacharage is launching businesses, salons, shops, hotels. Nduti is commercialising and accessing new markets. Momul is shifting mindset from the inside out. Rwanda is expanding land. Malawi has the fastest community diffusion of any cohort. Rather than producing a single uniform outcome, the programme unlocks whatever leverage point is most accessible in each local context, without changing the core curriculum.

What the cooperative comparison tells us

Each cooperative entered the programme with different starting conditions and has emerged with a distinct change signature, yet all four show significant movement across every key indicator. Gacharage's near-perfect budget adoption (99%) paired with the widest venture portfolio shows what financial discipline looks like when it fully takes root. Nduti's shift away from tea as primary income (down to 35%) is the most structurally significant. Momul's 17% no-change rate is the most honest data point from participants, and its high loan payoff rate (76%) suggests that those who did engage, went deep. Assophette's standout land expansion outcome reflects a Rwanda-specific livelihood strategy that the programme unlocked without prescribing it. Taken together, these four archetypes make the case that one programme curriculum can produce contextually relevant, measurable change across very different farming communities.

What the longitudinal data tells us

Across all four cohorts, the programme creates durable change that holds and compounds over time. In Kenya, Nduti income grew a further 79% between follow-up and endline, while Momul's ripple effect expanded from ×5 to ×6 — showing community diffusion still growing after training ended. In Rwanda, savings grew ×2.8 by endline and the ripple effect reached an extraordinary ×10. Malawi stands out with the highest ripple effect of all cohorts at ×13 at endline, despite income and savings dipping slightly from follow-up levels — a pattern consistent with post-peak stabilisation rather than regression. Across all countries, tea as a primary income source is declining as diversification takes hold, confirming that the programme is reshaping livelihoods, not just behaviours.
